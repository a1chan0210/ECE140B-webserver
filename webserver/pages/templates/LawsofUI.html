Laws of UI Design Examples

Hick's Law

League of Legends Client

The League of Legends client is a example of bad UI design with Hick's Law. There are two locations that look the same but only one is interactable. To pick the roles you want to play in League of Legends, you need to select them with the two circles at the bottom which I have circled in red. The two icons boxed in blue only shows the roles you have selected and you cannot interact with those icons. This change to the game client sparked some outrage in the community over the poor UI design. The 2 sets of icons that show the same information provide no extra benefit to the user experience but instead hinders it as it adds more confusion to the icons purpose, which is to select the roles you want to play in the game.

Jacob's Law

Microsoft Word and Google Docs

The relatively more recent google docs took advantage of Jacobs Law to develop a good UI for its product. The interface at the top is similar to its competitor which completes the same function, Microsoft Word. As many people at the time are used to using Microsoft Word and its interface, Google Doc's similar interface to Microsoft Word allowed an easy transition from customers that used Microsoft Word to using Google Docs.

Law of Proximity

Canvas

The Canvas service used in many universities creates a good UI using the Law of Proximity. It organizes things together in a way that makes sense. On the left, it has put together all the tabs you need for navigating the website. In the center of the home page, it places all your classes together so that it is easily accessable and you always know where to find your class information. On the right, it compiles a to do list of all the upcoming tasks and events.
